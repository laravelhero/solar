When homeowners add storage, the first question is always the same: *how many batteries do I need?* The honest answer is "it depends" — on what you want to keep running, and for how long. Here's how to think about it.

## Two backup philosophies

### Partial (essentials) backup
You back up only your **critical loads** — refrigerator, internet/router, a few lights and outlets, maybe a furnace fan. A single battery can run these for a **long time**, which is perfect for riding out typical outages without breaking the bank.

### Whole-home backup
You back up **everything**, including high-draw appliances like central AC, electric water heaters and well pumps. This requires **more capacity** — usually multiple stacked batteries — but means an outage is essentially invisible.

## The two numbers that matter

Battery sizing comes down to two things:

1. **Power (kW)** — how *much* you can run at once. AC compressors and well pumps have high startup surges, so whole-home backup needs more instantaneous power.
2. **Energy (kWh)** — how *long* you can run it. More kWh = more hours of runtime.

A single modern home battery typically offers around **5 kW of power and 13.5 kWh of energy** — enough to run essentials for many hours, but not enough to carry a full home with AC for a full day.

## How to size it: a simple method

1. **List your must-run loads** during an outage.
2. **Add up their daily energy use** in kWh (your installer models this precisely).
3. **Decide your target runtime** — a few hours, a full day, or multiple days.
4. **Match capacity:** multiply daily usage by target days, then size your battery bank accordingly.

A handy rule of thumb:

- **Essentials, long runtime:** 1 battery (~13.5 kWh)
- **Most of the home, ~1 day:** 2 batteries (~27 kWh)
- **Whole home incl. AC, 1–2 days:** 3 batteries (~40 kWh)

## Don't forget solar recharge

Here's the magic: unlike a generator, a solar-charged battery **refills every sunny day**. So even a moderately sized battery can carry you through a multi-day outage, because each day the sun tops it back up. Your panels and batteries form a self-sustaining loop.

## Start smart, scale later

Because modern systems are **modular**, a great strategy is to start with the capacity you need today and **add batteries later** as your budget allows or your needs grow (a new EV, a home addition, a medical device).

## The right size is the one modeled to your home

Generic advice only gets you so far. The precise answer comes from modeling your actual critical loads, outage history and solar production — which is exactly what a storage assessment delivers, so you buy the right amount of backup, not too much or too little.
