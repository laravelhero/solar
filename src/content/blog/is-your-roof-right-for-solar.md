Not every roof is created equal when it comes to solar. The good news: most American roofs are solid candidates, and even tricky ones often work with smart design. Here's how to evaluate yours.

## 1. Direction (orientation)

In the Northern Hemisphere, the sun tracks across the southern sky, so:

- **South-facing:** best — maximum daily production
- **West-facing:** excellent — produces into the valuable evening peak
- **East-facing:** very good — strong morning production
- **North-facing only:** least ideal, but modern high-efficiency panels can still make it viable

Most homes have multiple roof planes, so we design around the best-performing surfaces.

## 2. Shade

Shade is solar's biggest enemy. Watch for:

- Tall trees to the south or west
- Neighboring buildings
- Chimneys, vents and dormers

The fix is often simple: **module-level optimizers or microinverters** let each panel perform independently, so shade on one panel doesn't drag down the whole array. Light tree trimming can also unlock significant production.

## 3. Roof age and condition

Solar panels last **25+ years**, so your roof should be able to keep up. As a rule:

- **10+ years of life left:** install solar now
- **Less than 10 years left:** replace the roof first, or **bundle** a new roof with your solar so you only pay for labor and permitting once

## 4. Roof material

We install on virtually every material:

- **Asphalt shingle** — the easiest and most common
- **Metal standing-seam** — excellent; often clamp-mounted with no penetrations
- **Tile** (clay/concrete) — very doable with specialized hardware
- **Flat / membrane** — common on commercial and modern homes, using ballasted or tilted racking

## 5. Pitch and space

- **Pitch:** most pitches between 15° and 40° are ideal, but flat and steep roofs both work with the right racking.
- **Space:** a typical 6 kW system needs roughly 300–400 sq ft of usable roof.

## What if my roof isn't ideal?

You still have great options:

- **Ground-mount** systems if you have yard space
- **Solar carports** that also shade your vehicles
- **Community solar** — subscribe to a local solar farm with no rooftop install at all

## The only assessment that counts

This checklist gets you 80% of the way, but the definitive answer comes from a professional evaluation that uses satellite imagery and on-site shading analysis to model your roof precisely — and it's free.
