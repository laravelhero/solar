Your solar panels often produce more power than your home needs at midday — and less at night. What happens to that surplus, and how you're paid for it, is one of the most important factors in your solar savings.

## Net metering: the gold standard

Under **net metering**, your utility credits you at the **full retail rate** for every kilowatt-hour you export. Send 5 kWh to the grid at noon, pull 5 kWh back at night, and you've washed out — the grid effectively acted as a free battery.

This is the simplest and most generous arrangement, and many states still offer it at or near 1:1.

## Net billing: the newer model

As solar has grown, some utilities have shifted to **net billing** (net energy metering's successor). The difference: exported power is credited at a lower **export rate** — closer to the utility's wholesale cost — rather than the full retail rate.

That means the power you **use directly** from your panels is worth far more than the power you **export**. The strategy shifts from "export everything" to "**use as much of your own solar as possible**."

## California's NEM 3.0 in focus

California's **NEM 3.0** is the most prominent net-billing program. Under it:

- Export credits dropped substantially versus the old NEM 2.0
- Credits now **vary by time of day**, rewarding evening export when the grid needs it
- **Battery storage** becomes the key to capturing your system's full value

The takeaway for Californians: solar still pays — but **solar + storage** is now the design that maximizes returns.

## Why batteries are the answer under net billing

When exporting is worth less, storing your midday surplus and using it yourself in the expensive evening hours is worth *more*. A battery:

- Lets you **self-consume** nearly all your production
- **Shifts** stored energy into peak-rate windows
- Adds **backup power** as a bonus

## How to know what applies to you

Net-metering rules vary by **state and even by utility**. Some states are 1:1, some are tiered, some have moved to net billing. The right system design depends entirely on your local rules — which is exactly what a professional design accounts for.

## Bottom line

- **Net metering (1:1):** maximize array size; battery optional.
- **Net billing / NEM 3.0:** design storage-first to self-consume your power.

Either way, understanding how your utility credits you is the difference between a good solar investment and a great one.
