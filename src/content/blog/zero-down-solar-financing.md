The biggest myth about solar is that you need a pile of cash to get started. In 2026, the majority of homeowners go solar with **$0 down** — and start saving immediately. Here's how the financing actually works.

## Three ways to pay $0 down

### 1. Solar loan (most popular)
You **own** the system from day one and repay it in fixed monthly installments — no down payment required.

- **You claim the 30% federal tax credit**
- Rates from around **3.99%** on qualified credit
- Build equity in a real asset
- Payment often **lower than your old utility bill**

This is the best long-term value for most homeowners because *you* keep the incentives and the asset.

### 2. Solar lease
A third party owns the system on your roof and you **pay a fixed monthly amount** to use it.

- **No** tax credit (the owner claims it)
- **No** maintenance responsibility
- Predictable, often-lower monthly cost
- Good for those who can't use the tax credit

### 3. Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)
Similar to a lease, but instead of a fixed payment you **buy the power** the system produces at a set per-kWh rate, usually below the utility's.

- Pay only for what's produced
- No ownership or maintenance
- Locked-in rate protects against utility hikes

## The "lower than your bill" math

Here's why $0-down loans are so compelling. Say your electric bill is **$180/month**. A $0-down solar loan payment might be **$140/month** — and it *replaces* most of that utility bill rather than adding to it.

The result: you're **cash-flow positive from month one**, and once the loan is paid off, your power is essentially free for the remaining life of the system.

## Loan vs. lease/PPA: how to choose

- **Choose a loan if** you have tax liability to use the 30% credit and want to maximize lifetime savings and home value.
- **Choose a lease/PPA if** you can't use the tax credit, prefer zero maintenance responsibility, or want the simplest possible arrangement.

## Pre-qualify without the credit hit

Most solar lenders let you **pre-qualify with a soft credit check** that doesn't affect your score. In a few minutes you'll know your rate and monthly payment — before committing to anything.

## The bottom line

The real cost of *not* going solar is the rising utility bill you'll keep paying forever. With $0-down financing, the question shifts from "can I afford solar?" to "why am I still renting my power?"
